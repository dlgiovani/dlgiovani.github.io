# Brazil 2026 Presidential Race: Comparative Research Dossier — Lula, Flávio Bolsonaro, Renan Santos

*Updated edition — as of 30 May 2026. This version adds: global-context analysis for Lula's first terms; the welfare-dependency and statistics debates (corrected); public-sector wage growth; company bankruptcy/judicial-reorganization data; expanded coverage of the Lava Jato accusation set; strengthened COVID framing for the Bolsonaro presidency; and a precedent-based (Bukele/Milei) re-rating of Renan Santos's proposals.*

**Bottom line up front:** As of late May 2026, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) is the confirmed front-runner, leading every first- and second-round polling scenario; Senator Flávio Bolsonaro (PL) is the clear second-place force running explicitly as heir to his ineligible father Jair Bolsonaro, but his candidacy is a working hypothesis weakened by the May 2026 "Vorcaro/Banco Master" audio leak; and Renan Santos (Missão), the MBL founder, is a distant but rising third with a thin public record and no prior elected office.

## TL;DR
- **Lula leads but is not safe.** AtlasIntel/Bloomberg (19 May 2026) put Lula near 47% in the first round vs. Flávio ~34.3% (down ~5.4 pts after the Vorcaro leak); Genial/Quaest (13 May) showed a tighter race — Lula 39% / Flávio 33% first round, and a **technical tie in the runoff (Lula 42% / Flávio 41%)**. Datafolha (12–13 May) had them **tied at 45%** in the runoff. The pollsters disagree on margin, agree on order.
- **Context cuts both ways on the economy.** Lula's strong 2003–2010 numbers overlapped the **2003–2011 global commodity supercycle** (a structural tailwind); Jair Bolsonaro's weak 2019–2022 numbers were dominated by **COVID-19**. Neither candidate's mandate-era indicators should be read without that backdrop.
- **On "PT manipulates the statistics":** the **strong "systematic falsification" version is not established** (and is rejected by fact-checkers such as Aos Fatos), but **narrower critiques are defensible** — Dilma-era **price-suppression policy** that delayed inflation until the 2015 burst (10.67%), a **low official poverty line** that undercounted the poor, a headline unemployment rate that (by ILO standard) excludes the discouraged, and a **real institutional-independence concern at IBGE since the 2023 Pochmann appointment**. On welfare reach: ~9% report benefit *income*, but **~28% of Brazilians (and >50% in rural areas) live in a household receiving a social program** (IBGE 2023) — "approaching half" holds as household reach, not as "half live on government instead of work." See §2b.
- **Both front-runners carry serious, court-annulled-or-alleged corruption baggage, plus a shared 2026 scandal.** Lula's Lava Jato convictions were **annulled on procedural/jurisdictional and judge-bias grounds — not a merits acquittal**; the broader 2016–17 accusation set (Época's "13 cases") was prosecution evidence, mostly rejected or annulled. Flávio's "rachadinha" case was likewise **annulled procedurally, not on the merits.** The Banco Master/Vorcaro affair now touches **both** camps — all **[ALLEGED]**, none formally charged as of late May 2026.
- **Renan Santos is real but lightly documented.** Polling 3rd (~5–7% AtlasIntel; 2% Quaest), he is a first-time candidate (never elected) running a Bukele-style "prendeu, matou" platform; honest data gaps remain on his governing capacity.

> **A note on sourcing and method.** This dossier balances **left-leaning** (CartaCapital, Brasil de Fato, Intercept Brasil, ICL, Brasil 247), **center/mainstream** (G1, Folha de S.Paulo, UOL, Poder360, CNN Brasil, Metrópoles, Agência Brasil, Exame, ND Mais), and **right-leaning** (Gazeta do Povo, Veja, Diário do Poder) outlets, plus official/technical sources (IBGE, World Bank, IMF, UNDP, Banco Central, Ipea, Serasa Experian, STF, STJ, Senado, TSE). Outlet leanings are noted on contested claims. **The proposal-likelihood ratings (HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW), the structural critiques, and the competence/positioning tables are explicitly analytical judgments grounded in cited facts — not objective measurements.** Where data is genuinely unavailable, this is stated rather than invented.

> **On the user-provided poll (AtlasIntel/Bloomberg, 19 May 2026):** Registered at the TSE (BR-06939/2026), it surveyed 5,032 adults 13–18 May via "Atlas RDR" random digital recruitment (±1 pp, 95%). **Leaning/method caveats:** AtlasIntel (CEO Andrei Roman) is a private digital-recruitment pollster with a strong recent track record but a non-probabilistic online method that differs from in-home polling; it has at times shown larger Lula leads than face-to-face pollsters. **Poder360**, which hosts the PDF, is a center, Brasília-focused political outlet. # CANDIDATE 1 — Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT)

### 1. Profile
- **Full name:** Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. **Party:** PT (left). **Current office:** President of Brazil (39th president; third term since 1 January 2023). **[CONFIRMED]**
- **Age:** Born 27 October 1945, Caetés, Pernambuco — **80** in May 2026.
- **Timeline:** Metalworker → 1975 elected São Bernardo metalworkers' union president → 1978–1980 led the ABC strikes against the dictatorship; jailed ~31 days in 1980 → 10 Feb 1980 co-founded the PT, 1983 helped found the CUT → 1986 elected federal deputy → lost presidential bids 1989, 1994, 1998 → elected president 2002, reelected 2006 → terms 2003–2010 → 2018 Lava Jato conviction, barred, imprisoned **580 days** → 2019 released → 2021 convictions annulled → 2022 elected to a third term (50.9%) over incumbent Bolsonaro; inaugurated 1 Jan 2023 as the oldest person to take the office.

### 2. Previous Mandate(s)
**2003–2006 & 2007–2010.** *Achievements:* Bolsa Família creation/expansion, large real minimum-wage gains, major poverty/inequality reduction, peak international prestige. Average annual GDP growth ~**4.05%** (above the ~2.73% world average; Unicamp Institute of Economics); real GDP up ~**37%**; investment rose to ~**19% of GDP**; minimum-wage purchasing power +~**46%** (DIEESE/Unicamp). HDI rose from 0.669 (2000) toward **0.726 (high) by 2010** (UNDP). Left office with ~**80% approval** (CNI-Ibope, Dec 2010). *Failures:* the 2005 **mensalão** scandal (see §4); weak security/infrastructure.

> **Essential context — the commodity supercycle [analysis, well-supported]:** These two terms coincided almost exactly with the **2003–2011 global commodity supercycle**, an exceptional China-driven boom in iron ore, oil, soy and metals prices (World Bank; IMF *Finance & Development*, 2012; CEPR). Brazil — a major commodity exporter via Vale, Petrobras and agribusiness — was a structural beneficiary, and the Carnegie Endowment (2013), citing World Bank data, notes Latin America's middle class grew ~50% (103M in 2003 → 152M in 2009) on that windfall. *Analytical judgment:* a meaningful share of the first-government prosperity was external tailwind, not purely policy — a point critics press and the supercycle literature supports. The fair counter is that not every commodity exporter converted the windfall into comparable poverty reduction, so policy choices (cash transfers, real minimum-wage policy) also mattered. The honest reading is "both," and the boom's 2011 unwinding helped expose the model's limits under his successor.

**2023–present.** *Achievements:* Restored Bolsa Família, Minha Casa Minha Vida (tier 1, subsidies up to ~95%), Mais Médicos; passed the new fiscal framework and a landmark consumption-tax reform; began disinflation and early Selic cuts. IBGE: GDP **+3.4% (2024), +2.3% (2025)**; Folha found **66 of 99 indicators improved** in 2023; work income and the employed population hit record highs in 2025 (IBGE PNAD). *Failures:* worsening fiscal deficit and falling FDI (Folha); a late-2024 currency rout needing BC intervention; high real interest rates (Selic ~14%+ in 2025–26) capping growth; the Master/Vorcaro affair (§4b). Finance Ministry SPE projects **2.3% GDP / 3.6% IPCA for 2026**. HDI has largely plateaued near 0.76 (UNDP).

> **Company distress — bankruptcy & judicial-reorganization data [CONFIRMED, Serasa Experian].** Filings for **judicial reorganization (*recuperação judicial*)** hit successive all-time records under Lula 3: requests jumped ~36% in 2023, rose to **2,273 in 2024 (+61.8%, a record since the 2005 law)**, and reached **2,466 companies in 2025 (+13%, an all-time high)**, with **agribusiness (~30%)** the most-affected sector. *Counter-balancing fact, same source:* outright **bankruptcies (*falências*) FELL** — to **698 CNPJs in 2025 (−19% vs. 2024)**, roughly 61% below the 1,810 of 2012. Serasa attributes the reorganization surge primarily to the **restrictive Selic rate, selective credit, and agro/climate and dollarized-input shocks** — not to a single policy. *Honest framing:* this is a real corporate-stress signal, but the falling-bankruptcy figure cuts against a simple "economy collapsing" reading, and the dominant driver (high interest rates) is set by the autonomous Central Bank.

### 2b. Contested structural critiques [analysis — flagged, evenhanded]
The following are recurring critiques of the PT economic model and of "statistics manipulation." Several contain a verifiable core that is routinely **overstated into a falsification claim**; this section separates the two. *Overall finding:* the **strong version — "PT systematically falsifies IBGE data" — is not established and is rejected by fact-checkers** (e.g., **Aos Fatos**, which documents bolsonarista disinformation aimed at discrediting IBGE figures). The **defensible** critiques are narrower and concern (i) what headline metrics do and don't capture, (ii) threshold/definition choices, (iii) price-suppression *policy* that distorted measured inflation, and (iv) institutional-independence risk. Each is treated below.

- **How the unemployment rate is defined — a real caveat, not manipulation [CONFIRMED, technical].** Critics correctly note the headline *taxa de desocupação* can flatter the labor market, but the reason is the **international (ILO) definition IBGE uses**, not tampering: (a) anyone doing **any** paid activity — formal *or* informal — counts as **employed**, so receiving a benefit does not change one's status; and (b) people who **stopped looking for work ("desalentados"/discouraged)** leave the *labor force* entirely and are **excluded from the unemployment rate**, moving into "potential labor force" or "outside the labor force" (IBGE PNAD methodology; Banco Central study on *desalento*). This means the headline rate **understates labor-market slack** — which is precisely why IBGE *also* publishes the broader **composite underutilization rate** (unemployed + underemployed-by-hours + discouraged + potential force). *Net:* a fair critique of relying on the headline number alone, applicable to **every** government and consistent with global standards — not evidence of doctoring.
- **Welfare reach — your "≈50%" point, stated precisely [CONFIRMED].** The ~9.1% figure is *individuals reporting benefit income*; it understates **household reach**, which is the more relevant lens. Counting **people living in a household that receives any social program**, IBGE's *Síntese de Indicadores Sociais* puts the share at **~27.9% of Brazilians in 2023** (up from 25.8% in 2022), having **peaked at 36.8% in 2020** (pandemic emergency aid), and **over 51% in rural areas** (IBGE/Agência Gov, 2024). Because Bolsa Família is paid **per family** (≈20M titular families) and the benefit scales with the number of dependents, the count of *people touched* (~50M+) vastly exceeds the ~19.4M reporting benefit income, and CadÚnico registration reaches ≈94M (~44%). *So:* "approaching half" is accurate **as household reach and in rural Brazil**, though not as "half the country lives on government instead of work" nationally — most benefited households also have work income.
  - *The dependency debate, evenhanded:* **Right-leaning** outlets (Gazeta do Povo; ID Previdenciário, 2025) read the ~94M CadÚnico figure and ~R$0.5tn/yr social spending as structural dependency, sometimes as electorally self-interested. **World Bank counter-evidence is substantial:** no measurable "laziness effect" (*efeito preguiça*), **~70% of beneficiary-family income from work**, Bolsa Família **<25% of household income for 71% of families**, and an 8–10% benefit-loss-on-formalization rate (2nd-lowest of 30 OECD-compared countries) — a weak work disincentive (World Bank, 2021). *Net:* dependency is a legitimate concern at the margin and dominant in many small transfer-reliant municipalities, but the data do not support a "half the country chooses welfare over work" reading.
- **Poverty-line choice — a real debate that cuts *against* PT's headline gains [contested].** Brazil's **official** extreme-poverty line (long pegged near R$77/month, on a UN Millennium-Goals reference) was **low by international standards**, so fewer people counted as "extremely poor" than under **World Bank lines** (US$1.90, later US$2.15/day PPP). When the World Bank updated its lines in 2015 and IBGE later applied them, the recalculation **revealed *more* poverty than the official series had shown** — which is why right-leaning critics (Augusto Nunes / Veja) argued the government's favorable threshold had **undercounted ("hidden") the poor** (BBC Brasil, 2015; Veja). *Important framing:* this is a critique of **threshold selection** — a transparent, debatable methodological choice that pre- and post-dates PT — **not secret falsification**, and the underlying trend (large reduction in the 2000s; a real **reversal in the 2014–16 crisis**, when World Bank data show extreme poverty rising 2.8%→3.4% and poverty 7.4%→8.7%) is robust across lines (World Bank).
- **Administered-price suppression and distorted inflation — well-documented, mainly Dilma-era [CONFIRMED policy; "index falsification" framing rejected].** This is the strongest of the critiques. During **Dilma's first term (2011–2014)** the government **artificially held down administered prices** — especially **gasoline** (via Petrobras, which had ~3.77% IPCA weight, making the effect outsized) and **electricity** (via **MP 579**, Sept 2012) — to keep measured **IPCA** near the target (peer-reviewed analysis: *Revista de Economia Política*/SciELO; contemporaneous Folha de S.Paulo "malabarismo intervencionista," Valor "estratégia arriscada"). The suppressed adjustments were **released after the 2014 election** (the 2015 "tarifaço"), and inflation **burst to 10.67% in 2015**, contributing to the 2015–16 recession and hitting the poorest hardest (Época, "O PT contra os pobres," 2016). *Two crucial fairness points:* (1) this was **price-control *policy* that suppressed the underlying prices feeding the index — the IPCA itself was measured correctly, not "rigged"**; and (2) **fuel-price suppression is bipartisan** — Jair Bolsonaro did the same in 2022 (Correio Braziliense), and intervention recurs across governments. *Net:* a legitimate and damaging critique of *policy*, not proof of statistical fraud.
- **IBGE independence under Pochmann (2023–) — a real institutional concern, no proven falsification [reported].** Lula's **August 2023 appointment of Márcio Pochmann** — a PT-affiliated economist (ex-IPEA, ex-Fundação Perseu Abramo) — to head IBGE was controversial from the outset (reportedly made without Planning Minister Simone Tebet's prior knowledge) and triggered **repeated open letters from career staff and the IBGE union (Assibge)** in 2023–2026, alleging "centralizing/authoritarian" management, a secretive "**Fundação IBGE+**" that could capture private money (raising independence concerns), a push to bypass mainstream-media data releases, and the **2026 departure of the National Accounts coordinator** who oversees GDP (Metrópoles; CNN Brasil; Gazeta do Povo). *Honest framing:* these are documented **governance and independence-*risk* concerns**, and they legitimately "raise dúvidas" about impartiality — but there is **no established finding that poverty, inflation, or GDP data were actually manipulated** under Pochmann; the series remain in use by markets, the World Bank and the IMF. The concern is about safeguards, not a demonstrated breach.
- **Public-sector wage growth [CONFIRMED, with debate].** Real public-sector pay rose markedly over the PT era: Ipea finds federal/state/municipal public salaries grew **~23.5% over three decades while private-sector pay fell**, with the **gains concentrated 2003–2014** (Ipea, via Gazeta do Povo, 2019); active-personnel spending rose from **9.6% to 10.5% of GDP between 2004 and 2007**. The World Bank's *Um Ajuste Justo* finds a **67% federal public-private wage premium** (highest of 53 countries) and a 31% state premium; CNI/IMF data put total personnel spending at ~13.4% of GDP (2018), 6th-highest of 70+ countries. *Counter (Ipea; public-sector unions):* the trajectory is **not "explosive,"** sits within Fiscal Responsibility Law limits, and tracked private formal-sector expansion. *Net:* the wage growth and the public-private premium are real and a legitimate structural-cost critique; the "fiscally out-of-control" version is disputed by the same Ipea data critics cite.

> **Bottom line on "manipulation":** The honest, publishable synthesis is that **PT-era governments made policy and definitional choices that flattered some headline indicators** (price suppression that delayed inflation; a low official poverty line; reliance on a headline unemployment rate that excludes the discouraged) and that **the politicization risk at IBGE since 2023 is real and worth watching** — but the categorical claim that **the data themselves are systematically falsified is not supported** and is actively contested by fact-checkers. Present the specific, sourced critiques; avoid the blanket charge.

### 4. Corruption Scandals
**4a. Confirmed [CONFIRMED]:**
- **Mensalão (AP 470), scheme 2003–2005, STF 2012:** STF convicted senior PT figures for congressional vote-buying — ex-Chief of Staff **José Dirceu** (10y10m), Genoíno (~6y11m), Delúbio Soares (~8y11m). **Lula was never a defendant and was not convicted**; later delator claims that he knew are **[ALLEGED]**.
- **Lula personally:** has **no standing criminal conviction** today.

**The Lava Jato nuance [CONFIRMED facts]:** Moro convicted Lula (triplex, July 2017, 9y6m) and Hardt convicted him (sítio de Atibaia, Feb 2019, 12y11m); Lula served **580 days**. In 2021 the STF (a) ruled the **13th Federal Court of Curitiba lacked jurisdiction** (Fachin, confirmed by plenary), and (b) **7–4 declared Moro biased ("suspeito")**, Gilmar Mendes extending it to Atibaia. **These were annulled on procedural/jurisdictional and bias grounds — the STF did NOT rule the facts were false.** Re-prosecution attempts in Brasília failed (TRF-1 upheld rejection of Atibaia, July 2025; triplex archived). *Honest framing:* Lula's campaign calls this restored "innocence" — that is political characterization, not a merits acquittal.

> **The broader accusation set — Época's "13 cases" [ALLEGED / mostly rejected or annulled].** In May 2017, **Época** (Globo group, center-right; reporter Diego Escosteguy) ran a cover compiling the Lava Jato task force's case against Lula: **"~3,000 pieces of evidence, 13 cases, ~R$82 million" in alleged undue benefits**, spanning charges of corruption, money laundering, criminal organization, influence-peddling and obstruction, amplified by the Odebrecht plea bargains. *Critical framing required:* these were **prosecution accusations and evidence asserted by the MPF/Polícia Federal, not 13 convictions.** Over time, courts **rejected the denúncia in several of them** (including cases involving family members and the Zelotes/automaker matter, where the MPF itself requested acquittal), and the only two that produced convictions (triplex, Atibaia) were **annulled** on the jurisdictional + bias grounds above. The task force's framing of Lula as the scheme's "comandante máximo" was central to the prosecution's narrative but was **never sustained to a final conviction**, and the task force's own conduct was later discredited (the "Vaza Jato" leaks; the STF's bias ruling against Moro; lead prosecutor Deltan Dallagnol later lost his congressional mandate). *Net:* a large, serious accusation set existed and is part of the public record — but under this dossier's labeling rules it remains **[ALLEGED]/annulled**, the same standard applied to Flávio's rachadinha.

# CANDIDATE 2 — Flávio Bolsonaro (PL)

### 1. Profile
- **Full name:** Flávio Nantes Bolsonaro. **Party:** PL (right). **Current office:** Senator for Rio de Janeiro (2019–2027); 3rd Secretary of the Senate board.
- **Age:** Born 30 April 1981, Resende, RJ — **45**. Eldest son of Jair Bolsonaro.
- **Candidacy status — WORKING HYPOTHESIS:** Announced as his father's chosen successor 5 Dec 2025; launched pre-candidacy at CPAC Dallas 28 Feb 2026. Runs on Jair's legacy because **Jair is ineligible until 2030** (TSE conviction + STF coup conviction), pledging "ampla, geral e irrestrita" amnesty for his father.
- **Timeline:** Elected state deputy (Alerj) at 21 (2002, office 2003); **four terms 2003–2018** → 2016 ran for Rio mayor (PSC), 4th (~14%) → 2018 elected Senator with **4,380,418 votes**, most-voted in Rio history (PSL→PL).

### 2. Previous Mandate(s)
**Own Senate mandate (2019–present):** Author/co-author of dozens of security bills/PECs, but per Folha/Estado de Minas only **two co-authored measures enacted** (IPVA exemption for 20+yr vehicles; microcredit law); 2024 floor rapporteur for the law ending temporary prison leaves. High electoral strength, **thin legislative conversion**.

**Father's presidency (2019–2022) — the legacy he runs on:**
> **Essential context — this was the COVID-19 presidency.** Three of the four years were dominated by the pandemic, which is inseparable from the economic record. The **2020 GDP contraction (~−3.3%)** and the **14.9% unemployment peak (early 2021)** were primarily pandemic-driven (IBGE; World Bank) — the mirror image of Lula's favorable-commodity-cycle caveat. Any fair read of Bolsonaro's numbers must hold COVID constant, just as Lula's first-term numbers must be read against the boom.

*Achievements (Gazeta do Povo, right):* 2019 pension reform; BC autonomy; sanitation/railway frameworks, concessions/privatizations; COVID **Auxílio Emergencial** widely credited as effective income cushioning; Auxílio Brasil. *Failures:* one of the world's worst COVID death tolls (660,000+); conflicts with the Judiciary/Congress/governors; rising Amazon deforestation; record 2021–22 inflation; weak growth. Left outlets (CUT, Brasil de Fato) call the pension reform anti-poor. *Indicators:* avg GDP growth **~1.12%**, **below** the ~1.95% world average (Unicamp) — 2020 ~−3.3%, 2021 ~+4.8%, 2022 ~+3.0% (World Bank). Unemployment peaked **14.9% (early 2021)**, fell to ~8.9% by mid-2022 (IBGE). Inflation double-digit in 2021, ~6% in 2022 after fuel-tax cuts. Minimum-wage purchasing power **−~26%** (DIEESE/Unicamp). Poverty/inequality hit series-worst in 2021 (IBGE PNAD). *Note on bankruptcies:* outright **falências declined for three consecutive years (2020–2022)** before rising again from 2023 (Serasa) — consistent with pandemic-era credit support and emergency measures.

### 3. Government Proposals / Campaign Positions
*Analytical judgments; several positions inferred from his Senate record and statements (Gazeta do Povo, PlatôBR) given the absence of a finalized platform.*

| Area | Position | Likelihood | Justification |

# METHODOLOGY NOTE
**Proposal-likelihood ratings (HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW)** were assigned by triangulating four criteria: (1) **track record** — has the candidate or movement delivered similar policy before; (2) **stated commitment and specificity** — vague or early-stage proposals are rated lower; (3) **political feasibility** — control of the relevant lever (executive vs. state-led security vs. constitutional supermajorities) and the composition of a conservative Congress; and, new to this edition, (4) **comparative real-world precedent** — where an analogous program has been implemented abroad with measurable results (e.g., Bukele on security, Milei on macro-stabilization), the *plausibility of pursuit and partial delivery* is raised, but only to the extent the enabling conditions (above all a legislative majority) are reproducible in Brazil. Because Missão has essentially no congressional bench, the precedent raises Renan's security and economy ratings modestly (to MEDIUM and LOW–MEDIUM) rather than to HIGH. These ratings are **interpretive judgments, not forecasts.**

**Competence and positioning** were assessed against the documented record only: executive experience by time in office; legislative effectiveness by enacted-law conversion (Folha/Estado de Minas for Flávio); crisis management by observed performance; team quality by known advisor/minister profiles; and public trust by cross-pollster rejection (Quaest). Renan Santos is marked **N/A** where no governing record exists — a deliberate refusal to manufacture an assessment from absence of data. **Contested structural and "statistics-manipulation" critiques** (§2b) were handled with a three-way test: a claim is reported as **fact** only if confirmed by primary/official data; as a **defensible critique** if grounded in peer-reviewed or mainstream-sourced policy analysis (e.g., administered-price suppression; poverty-line choice; the ILO-standard limits of headline unemployment; IBGE-independence concerns since 2023); and as **unsupported** if it asserts systematic data falsification, which available evidence and fact-checkers (Aos Fatos) do not sustain. Each critique is paired with its strongest counter-evidence (Ipea, World Bank) and its sources' leanings are disclosed, since much of this material originates in right-leaning opinion columns (Veja, Gazeta do Povo) whose framing is noted as such.

**Sources and limitations.** Economic figures draw on IBGE, World Bank, IMF, UNDP, Banco Central, Ipea, the Finance Ministry's SPE, and Serasa Experian (bankruptcy/reorganization), supplemented by academic analysis (Unicamp Institute of Economics, left-of-center, noted). Legal facts rely on STF, STJ, Senado archives and legal press. Polling uses the user-provided AtlasIntel/Bloomberg PDF cross-checked against Datafolha and Genial/Quaest; **pollsters diverge on margins** (online recruitment vs. in-home), and all are snapshots. Two structural limitations apply: (a) Flávio's candidacy and platform are not finalized, so several positions are inferred; and (b) Renan Santos's public record is thin and partly drawn from profile journalism. Contested claims are labeled and outlet leanings disclosed; where verification failed, the dossier says so.

# CAVEATS
- **Flávio's candidacy is a working hypothesis**, and his platform is unfinalized; several of his "positions" are inferred.
- **Mandate-era economics must be read in context:** Lula's 2003–2010 numbers had a commodity-boom tailwind; Bolsonaro's 2019–2022 numbers were dominated by COVID-19. Crediting/blaming either leader without that backdrop is misleading.
- **On statistics "manipulation":** the blanket "PT falsifies the data" claim is **not supported** and is contested by fact-checkers; the **defensible, sourced critiques** are narrower — price-suppression *policy* (mainly Dilma, 2011–14, bipartisan), poverty-line/threshold choices, the ILO-standard limits of headline unemployment, and **institutional-independence risk at IBGE since the 2023 Pochmann appointment** (a watch-item, not a proven breach). On welfare: ~9% report benefit income, but **~28% (and >50% rural) live in a benefited household** (IBGE 2023).
- **The Banco Master/Vorcaro affair is active and bilateral** — it implicates figures near both Lula and Flávio but has produced **no indictment of either** as of late May 2026.
- **Lula's Lava Jato annulments (and the broader "13 cases" accusation set) and Flávio's rachadinha annulment were procedural, not merits acquittals** — a distinction routinely flattened by partisans on both sides.
- **Renan Santos's record is genuinely thin**; his corruption section is empty (no office held), his only documented financial issue is a contested civil/fiscal debt, and his governing capacity is untested.
- **The Bukele/Milei precedent is real but double-edged:** measurable results abroad, achieved with legislative majorities Renan lacks, and at contested human-rights and social costs. Academic economic framing (Unicamp) leans left-of-center; pandemic-era data carry methodological breaks.
